Legality of Scraping

May 12, 2017

During my first week at Flatiron School, I was briefly introduced to the topic of web scraping using the Ruby Gem Nokogiri. For those who do are not familiar with web scraping, it is extracting data from a website to use it however the end user wants to use that information. Eg: Real Estate Listings gathering, collecting financial data, collecting different prices for products...ect. While I was playing around with this newly discovered feature and extracting data from websites, I had to ask myself what if I were to use this data for my future website? It almost felt like it was stealing from someone else’s work to make it my own. This is where the question I had came to be: Is scraping Legal? I decided to do a little more research on regarding the legality of web scraping. It turns out that web scraping is a grey area that has being going back and forth in the courts over the years.

eBay v. Bidder's Edge

100 F.Supp.2d 1058 (N.D. Cal. 2000)

The first case challenging web scraping came to be in the year 2000 when eBay sued Bidder’s Edge for scraping data displaying their auction listings on their website. Ebay used the Trespass to Chattels Doctrine arguing that Bidder’s Edge was ignoring repeated requests to stop using crawlers(bots used for automatically scraping the website.) Ebay also argued that the use of crawlers by Bidder’s Edge was interfering with their servers. Bidder’s Edge argued that their use of crawlers only generated a slight interference with Ebay’s servers(80,000–100,000 hits per day, representing only 1 ½ percent of overall activities on Ebay’s website). In the end it was ruled in favor of Ebay basically stating that even though the inference had a small impact on Ebay’s site, other scrapers could come along and negativity affect the site. A preliminary injunction was issued as the result of the ruling.

Intel Corp. v. Hamidi

30 Cal. 4th 1342 (2003)

The Trespass to Chattels Doctrine ruling was only to be overturned a few years later in 2003 in the Intel Corp. v. Hamidi case. It was ruled that Trespass to Chattels could not extend to the context of computers if no actual damage to personal property occurred. For many years after this ruling, there were a few more cases taking legal action against scraping, however the courts would consistently shoot them down.

Facebook v. Power Ventures

844 F. Supp. 2d 1025 (ND California 2012)

It wasn’t until 2009 when things start to take a turn in the other direction when Facebook sued Power Ventures for copyright infringement. Facebook claimed that Power Ventures scraping their content was a violation of their Terms of Service specifically disallowing scraping data from their website. It was ruled in Facebook’s favor since their Terms of Service did not allow for scraping.

QVC Inc. v. Resultly LLC

No. 14-06714 (E.D. Pa 2014)

However, the tide would once again turn in the QVC Inc. v. Resultly case filed by QVC against Resultly in 2014. QVC claimed that Resultly were aggressively using crawlers to scrap their site to the point where it would cause QVC’s servers to shut down causing millions in revenue. In short, the court ruled that Resultly was not intentionally causing harm and QVC should do more to protect their site from crawlers.

The legality of web scraping is still quite murky and it appears that legal action for scraping is quite rare to the point where your everyday common scraping use would ever be bought to court. However, a scraper should take the following precautions to not only avoid any legal actions against them, but also out of courtesy for the website they are scraping from.

  • The content being scraped is not copyright protected
  • The act of scraping does not burden the services of the site being scraped
  • The scraper does not violate the Terms of Use of the site being scraped
  • The scraper does not gather sensitive user information
  • The scraped content adheres to fair use standards
